\title{Offline Satellite Image Captioning for Contested ISR:\\
Fine-Tuning SmolVLM on Multi-Reference Aerial Imagery}

\author{
\IEEEauthorblockN{Justin Williams, Kishor Datta Gupta, Roy George, Mrinmoy Sarkar}
\IEEEauthorblockA{Clark Atlanta University \quad \texttt{justinwilliamstech693@gmail.com}}
}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Satellite imagery analysis in contested or communication-denied environments requires
intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance (ISR) systems capable of generating natural-language
descriptions \emph{entirely on-device}, without cloud infrastructure or high-bandwidth uplinks.
We present an offline satellite captioning system based on \textbf{SmolVLM-Base} (256M parameters)
fine-tuned with Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) on a curated corpus of \textbf{9,828 geo-referenced
overhead images} with multi-reference natural-language annotations. Training employs a
\emph{random multi-reference collation} strategy---sampling one caption from multiple human-authored
references per training step---to promote diverse, non-template descriptions. The model fits in
\textbf{$<$512\,MB} of RAM, runs on CPU-only hardware, and produces semantically accurate scene
descriptions. Fine-tuning achieves \textbf{CIDEr 89.4} (6$\times$ over zero-shot baseline) and
\textbf{BLEU-4 18.6} at 1.8\,s per image on a Raspberry Pi~5 CPU with 140\,MB peak memory,
enabling practical offline ISR captioning for GPS-denied and communication-contested environments.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
satellite image captioning, offline ISR, edge VLM, LoRA, remote sensing, GPS-denied
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Modern ISR platforms generate satellite imagery at rates exceeding the bandwidth available in
contested electromagnetic environments. Analysts cannot be in the loop when communications are
degraded; the platform must itself produce structured intelligence products from raw sensor data.
Natural-language captioning---describing scene content, asset categories, and spatial relationships
in plain text---is a compact, human-readable output that can be transmitted in a few hundred bytes
and consumed by downstream automated reasoning systems.

Existing satellite captioning systems~\cite{rsicd,ucm,sydney} rely on large models (BLIP-2,
LLaVA, InstructBLIP) that require GPU servers or cloud inference, making them impractical for
forward-deployed edge platforms with 2--8\,W power budgets. We fine-tune SmolVLM~\cite{smolvlm},
a 256M-parameter vision--language model, specifically for satellite imagery.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \textbf{fine-tuned SmolVLM} checkpoint for satellite image captioning, trained on
          8,734 geo-referenced images with multi-reference annotations (Sec.~\ref{sec:dataset}).
    \item A \textbf{random multi-reference collation strategy} that exposes the model to diverse
          phrasings during training (Sec.~\ref{sec:method}).
    \item A \textbf{deployment profile} at $<$512\,MB memory and $<$8\,W power draw on CPU-only
          embedded hardware (Sec.~\ref{sec:deployment}).
    \item An \textbf{evaluation} against BLEU, METEOR, and CIDEr with comparison to a zero-shot
          BLIP-2 baseline (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Remote sensing captioning.}
Datasets for satellite captioning include RSICD~\cite{rsicd} (10,921 images), UCM-Captions~\cite{ucm}
(2,100 images), and Sydney-Captions~\cite{sydney} (613 images). Methods range from CNN+LSTM
encoders~\cite{lstm_rs} to transformer-based approaches~\cite{rs_transformer}. Recent work
leverages large VLMs zero-shot~\cite{skysense}. Ours is the first sub-300M-parameter VLM
fine-tuned for satellite captioning targeting CPU-only inference.

\textbf{Lightweight VLMs.}
SmolVLM~\cite{smolvlm}, MobileVLM~\cite{mobilevlm}, and moondream~\cite{moondream} reduce
VLM inference to sub-2\,GB memory. None have been evaluated on satellite imagery.

\textbf{Multi-reference training.}
Multiple reference captions per image are standard in COCO~\cite{coco} (5 per image) and
remote sensing datasets~\cite{rsicd}. Random per-batch reference sampling~\cite{rand_caption}
exposes the model to more phrasing diversity.

\section{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

The training corpus contains \textbf{9,828 geo-referenced overhead images} split into 8,734
training and 1,094 validation samples. Images are JPEG tiles spanning urban, peri-urban,
agricultural, forested, coastal, and arid terrain. Each image is paired with \textbf{multiple
reference captions} describing scene content, dominant land-use category, key object types
(vehicles, buildings, runways, water bodies), and spatial organization.

All samples share a fixed instruction:
\begin{quote}
\small\texttt{USER: <image> Describe this satellite image in detail. ASSISTANT:}
\end{quote}

\textbf{Multi-Reference Collation.}
At each training step, one reference caption is randomly sampled from the per-image reference set.
This exposes the model to all phrasings across epochs, preventing collapse to a single template.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\textbf{Base Model.}
We use \textbf{SmolVLM-Base}~\cite{smolvlm}: a SigLIP visual encoder paired with a SmolLM-2
language decoder, totaling approximately 256M parameters.

\textbf{Fine-Tuning.}
We apply LoRA ($r=32$, $\alpha=64$, dropout 0.05) to the attention and feed-forward projection
matrices of the language decoder. The visual encoder stays frozen; only the language decoder is
updated, reducing trainable parameters to $\approx$12M (4.7\% of total).

\textbf{Training Details.}
AdamW (lr$=2\times10^{-4}$, cosine decay, 100-step warmup); 1 epoch; batch size 8; gradient
clipping at 1.0; max sequence length 2,048 tokens; cross-entropy loss over caption tokens only.
Training completes in $<$2 hours on a single NVIDIA A100-80GB.

\section{Deployment}
\label{sec:deployment}

The LoRA adapter is merged into the base weights post-training and optionally quantized to GGUF Q4
for CPU-only inference.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Operational relevance.}
A satellite captioning model that fits within 140\,MB and runs at 1.8\,s per image is deployable
on small UAVs, portable intelligence terminals, and vehicle-mounted processing units. The text
output compresses to $<$1\,KB per image for transmission over low-bandwidth SATCOM links.

\textbf{Multi-reference training.}
Random per-batch reference sampling is a key design choice. Models trained with a fixed reference
quickly overfit to a single caption style; the multi-reference strategy produces more natural,
varied output and generalizes better to novel scene configurations.

\textbf{Limitations.}
Metrics are reference-based and do not capture operational utility directly. Change detection
(temporal comparison of multi-date imagery) is not addressed. The model does not produce bounding
boxes or structured geospatial outputs.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented an offline satellite captioning system based on SmolVLM-Base fine-tuned with LoRA
on 9,828 geo-referenced overhead images. The resulting model fits in 140\,MB, runs at 1.8\,s per
image on a CPU-only Raspberry Pi~5, and achieves CIDEr 89.4---a 6$\times$ improvement over
zero-shot baseline and substantially better than BLIP-2 zero-shot at 17$\times$ fewer parameters.
This work demonstrates the viability of lightweight offline VLMs for ISR captioning in GPS-denied,
communication-contested environments.
