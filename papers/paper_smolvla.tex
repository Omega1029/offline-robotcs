\title{SmolVLA: Lightweight On-Device Vision--Language--Action\\
for Autonomous Mobile Robots in GPS-Denied Environments}

\author{
\IEEEauthorblockN{Justin Williams, Kishor Datta Gupta, Roy George, Mrinmoy Sarkar}
\IEEEauthorblockA{Clark Atlanta University \quad \texttt{justinwilliamstech693@gmail.com}}
}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We present \textbf{SmolVLA}, a lightweight Vision--Language--Action (VLA) system that enables
autonomous mobile robots to reason about visual scenes and produce structured motion commands
entirely on-device---without GPS, cloud connectivity, or a dedicated GPU. SmolVLA fine-tunes
SmolVLM-Base (256M parameters) with Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) on a dataset of 15,883 paired
RGB frames and ROS~2 action commands collected via teleoperation on a TurtleBot~4. The resulting
policy runs at \textbf{1.2\,s per query} on a Raspberry Pi~5 CPU at \textbf{6.5\,W} average
power---a 37$\times$ parameter reduction over 7B-parameter VLA backbones with no dedicated
inference accelerator. We further study three design axes: (i)~\emph{temporal context}, where a
short frame history improves sequential decision consistency; (ii)~\emph{action chunking}, where
predicting a fixed-length action subsequence reduces query frequency and smooths trajectories;
and (iii)~\emph{dataset balancing}, where correcting the action-command distribution improves
generalization to underrepresented maneuvers. Dataset balancing lifts overall action accuracy
from 77.9\% to \textbf{85.2\%}. SmolVLA demonstrates real-time closed-loop navigation on a
physical TurtleBot~4 with ROS~2 via \texttt{/cmd\_vel}, and serves as an open prototype for
edge-deployable VLA research in GPS- and communication-denied environments.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
vision-language-action, edge robotics, on-device inference, LoRA, action chunking, GPS-denied

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Autonomous mobile robots operating in denied environments---where GPS signals are unavailable
and cloud compute is unreachable---require onboard intelligence capable of closed-loop perception
and control. Large Vision--Language--Action (VLA) models such as OpenVLA~\cite{openvla} and
$\pi_0$~\cite{pi0} offer strong generalization through large-scale pre-training, but their
7B-parameter backbones demand tens of gigabytes of memory and tens of watts of GPU power,
precluding deployment on platforms like the TurtleBot~4.

The alternative---task-specific controllers---sacrifices the generalization that language
grounding provides. We ask whether a \emph{smaller} VLM, fine-tuned on a modest dataset, can
bridge this gap: retaining language-conditioned reasoning while fitting within the compute
envelope of embedded hardware.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{SmolVLA}, an end-to-end pipeline that fine-tunes SmolVLM-Base (256M
          parameters) with LoRA on 15,883 teleoperated TurtleBot~4 demonstrations and deploys
          the policy on a Raspberry Pi~5 at 1.2\,s/query, 6.5\,W (Sec.~\ref{sec:method}).
    \item A \textbf{temporal context ablation} showing that conditioning on a history of $k$
          recent frames improves sequential action consistency (Sec.~\ref{sec:variants}).
    \item An \textbf{action chunking variant} that predicts a fixed-length action subsequence
          per query, reducing query frequency and smoothing trajectories
          (Sec.~\ref{sec:variants}).
    \item A \textbf{dataset balancing study} demonstrating that class-balanced sampling
          improves generalization to underrepresented maneuvers
          (Sec.~\ref{sec:variants}).
    \item A \textbf{ROS~2 integration} that publishes predicted commands to
          \texttt{/cmd\_vel}, closing the loop on a physical TurtleBot~4
          (Sec.~\ref{sec:method}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Large VLA models.}
OpenVLA~\cite{openvla} and OpenVLA-OFT~\cite{openvlaoft} fine-tune a 7B-parameter
vision--language backbone to produce robot actions. $\pi_0$~\cite{pi0} combines a VLM backbone
with a diffusion action head. These achieve strong performance but require GPU servers. SmolVLA
targets the opposite end: 256M parameters on a CPU-only embedded board.

\textbf{Lightweight VLMs.}
SmolVLM~\cite{smolvlm} and MobileVLM~\cite{mobilevlm} demonstrate that vision--language
understanding can be retained at sub-billion parameter scales. We build directly on SmolVLM-Base.

\textbf{Temporal context.}
Temporal models in robot learning---LSTM-augmented policies~\cite{lstm_policy} and
transformers over observation histories~\cite{gato}---improve sequential decision quality.
We apply this principle to SmolVLA via multi-image prompting.

\textbf{Action chunking.}
Action Chunking with Transformers (ACT)~\cite{act} demonstrates that predicting a chunk of
future actions reduces compounding error. SmolVLA-chunked adapts this to a VLM text-output policy.

\section{System Architecture}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Data Collection}
We collected $N=15{,}883$ paired observations by teleoperating a TurtleBot~4 in indoor
environments. At each timestep, the robot's RGB frame (640$\times$480) was paired with a
structured action label of the form \texttt{<direction>\_<speed>\_<duration>}
(e.g., \texttt{forward\_0.2\_3.0s}). The action vocabulary covers six primitives:
\texttt{forward}, \texttt{backward}, \texttt{left}, \texttt{right}, \texttt{stop}, and
\texttt{rotate}.

\subsection{Model}
We fine-tune \textbf{SmolVLM-Base}~\cite{smolvlm} (256M parameters; SigLIP visual encoder +
SmolLM-2 language decoder) using LoRA ($r=32$, $\alpha=64$, dropout 0.05) applied to attention
and feed-forward matrices. The input prompt is:
\begin{quote}
\small\texttt{USER: <image> What action should the robot take? ASSISTANT:}
\end{quote}
Training uses cross-entropy loss over action tokens, with images resized to 384$\times$384.

\subsection{Training}
We train for 10 epochs with AdamW (lr$=2\times10^{-4}$, cosine schedule, 100-step warmup),
batch size 8, gradient clipping at 1.0, on a single NVIDIA A100.

\subsection{ROS~2 Integration}
At deployment, the LoRA adapter is merged into the base model and optionally quantized to
GGUF Q4 for CPU inference. The inference node subscribes to \texttt{/camera/image\_raw},
formats the prompt, parses the action string, and publishes to \texttt{/cmd\_vel}:
\[
\texttt{Camera} \to \texttt{SmolVLA} \to \texttt{Parser} \to \texttt{/cmd\_vel} \to \texttt{Motors}
\]

\section{Variants}
\label{sec:variants}

\textbf{Temporal Context.}
The base SmolVLA processes a single frame per query. The temporal variant concatenates the
$k$ most-recent frames into a multi-image prompt. We evaluate $k \in \{1,2,4\}$ and report
action consistency on multi-step navigation corridors.

\textbf{Action Chunking.}
The chunked variant predicts a semicolon-delimited sequence of $C$ consecutive actions per
query. The robot executes the full chunk open-loop before the next query, reducing query
rate by $C$ and amortizing latency.

\textbf{Dataset Balancing.}
The raw dataset is skewed: \texttt{forward} commands exceed 60\%. The balanced variant
resamples to equal class frequency, improving rare-maneuver accuracy.

Dataset balancing improves overall accuracy by $+7.3$ points, primarily by recovering
underrepresented turning maneuvers ($+12$--$14$ points on left/right/rotate).

\subsection{Temporal Context}
Providing a 2-frame history ($k=2$) improves sequential action consistency by approximately
$+8$ points over the single-frame baseline on a 30-step navigation corridor. $k=4$ provides
marginal additional gain at $4\times$ the context length.

\subsection{Action Chunking}
Chunking at $C=4$ reduces query frequency from $\sim$0.83\,Hz to $\sim$3.3\,Hz effective
throughput while maintaining comparable trajectory quality. Open-loop chunk execution introduces
small drift on curved paths but significantly reduces per-step jitter on straight corridors.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

SmolVLA demonstrates that a 256M-parameter VLM fine-tuned with LoRA on a 15K-sample dataset
can serve as a functional on-device robot policy at CPU-only embedded hardware. The 37$\times$
parameter reduction over 7B-parameter VLAs comes at the cost of generalization: SmolVLA is
fine-tuned for a specific robot and action vocabulary, while large VLAs generalize zero-shot.
In GPS-denied or communication-denied mission environments, a specialized, reliable, offline-
capable policy is preferable to a powerful but cloud-dependent one.

\textbf{Limitations.} The action vocabulary is discrete and structured. Continuous
end-effector control would require a regression head or finer tokenization. The 1.2\,s latency
is adequate for slow navigation but too slow for reactive obstacle avoidance; the GGUF Q4
variant at 0.72\,s/query is more practical.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented SmolVLA, a lightweight on-device VLA for autonomous mobile robots in GPS- and
communication-denied environments. By fine-tuning a 256M-parameter VLM with LoRA on 15,883
teleoperation demonstrations, we achieve functional closed-loop navigation on a Raspberry Pi~5
at 1.2\,s/query, 6.5\,W, and sub-512\,MB memory. Dataset balancing, temporal context, and
action chunking each provide complementary improvements with no architecture change, raising
overall action accuracy to 85.2\%.
